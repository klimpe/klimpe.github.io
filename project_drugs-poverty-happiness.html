1. Ask

  • What topic are you exploring?
    The drug consumption and drug-related crimes at the country level, its relation to poverty and percieved happiness.
  • What is the problem you are trying to solve?
    The goal is to inform political decision-makers about what maximizes the happiness of the population.
  • What metrics will you use to measure your data to achieve your objective?
    Number of drug-related crimes (posession and dealing) per capita, happiness score, average rate of drug use during the last year
  • Who are the stakeholders?
    Political decision-makers.
  • Who is your audience?
    Political leaders and general public.
  • Questions:
    • What is the relationship between factors like happiness and poverty and drug-related behaviour?
    • Which parts of the world succeeded to maximize happiness of their citizens? What kind drug policies could be linked to that?
    • Which regions struggle with poverty and how does this reflect on their drug consumption?

2. Prepare

Finding the Datasets

The data comes from 4 different sources.

  • World Drug Report 2022 (WDR 2022), provided by United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC)
    • Drug-related offences
    • Last year usage ratios per drug type
  • Population by Country - 2020 from Kaggle
    • Population
  • World Bank Poverty and Inequality Platform (PIP) poverty data made available by Our World in Data on GitHub
    • Extreme poverty ratio
  • World Happiness Report (2019) from Kaggle
    • Happiness score
    • Happiness rank

4. Analysis

Types of Visualizations

Metrics chosen for this project such as rate of possession-related offences, trafficking-related offences and total rate of drug related offences can tell us something about illegal drug market and societies' relations to it, particularly if they are associated with such metrics as poverty rate or happiness score.

Maps

To begin to make sense of the data, I represented drug-related crime rates along with the percentage of people living in extreme poverty, both broken down by country, on a world map. Two types of offences are (1) related to possession and use and (2) related to trafficking. Along with total drug offence rate, they gave rise to three maps shown in the next section:

  • Total Drug Offences vs. Poverty
  • Use Offences vs. Poverty
  • Trafficking Offences vs. Poverty

The offence rate is represented by size of circles. The poverty rate is indicated by the color of circles. Blue corresponds to 0% (or to missing data), purple and cold pinkish colors to intermediate values between blue and yellow, which corresponds to 0.9%, orange corresponds to about 30% and bright red to 82% of the country's population living with under $2.15 a day.

Scatter plots

Another type of plot I found useful is a scatter plot of rate of a drug-related metric on the x-axis and of the inverse of extreme poverty percentage on the y-axis (so that the poorest are shown towards the bottom on the plot), using logarithmic scales on both axes. They also include the happiness score metric, color-coding the reported level of happiness in the presented countries with lowest happiness scores shown in bright red and highest in bright green. In the context of police data, three such plots have been created:

  • Drug Offences vs. Poverty and Happiness
  • Use Offences vs. Poverty and Happiness
  • Trafficking Offences vs. Poverty and Happiness

Analysis of Police Data

On the Use Offences vs. Poverty and Total Drug Offences vs. Poverty maps, we can clearly see a cluster of high rate of registered use offences and total offences in the rich European countries.

In absolute numbers in our data European countries and North America, richer parts of the world, which represent 33.3% of the countries in the dataset, account for 447 931 trafficking offences amounting to 53.2% of world's trafficking cases.

Usage and possession are more consistently reprimanded in the European part of the world, from 0.07% in Bulgaria to enormous 2.1% in Andorra. By contrast, in poor Sub-Saharan African countries, the rate of police actions against drug users and drug trafficking is very low: from 0.00007% in Ghana to 0.008% in the Central African Republic of offences being prosecuted. There are, however, some exceptions in cases of Zimbabwe, Botswana, Sudan and Zambia, where drug offence rate is higher. There, it ranges between 0.025% and 0.056% per person.

On the map Trafficking Offences vs. Poverty and on the scatter plot Trafficking Offences vs. Poverty and Happiness we can see some poorer countries from other regions joining Europe. Indeed, if we focus exclusively on drug trafficking offences, filtering out the punished use, the geography widens to include some developing countries including Colombia, Dominican Republic, Chile, Peru, Zambia, Tunisia, and Malaysia.

The plots Drug Offences vs. Poverty and Happiness and Trafficking vs. Poverty and Happiness reveal a negative correlation between registered drug-related offences and poverty, in other words, as we have already seen on the maps, there are way less offences in poor countries. Inversely, the lower the poverty rate, the more offences are registered.

Happiness score

Concurrently, if we focus on the happiness score, we will see that the plots also seem to show a trend associating most of the "happier" countries, mostly European, but also Israel and Morocco), with higher percentage of drug offences. Furthermore, countries with less than 1% of poverty and more than 0.5% of trafficking rate or more than 0.2% of total drug offence rate tend to have the highest happiness score. At the same time, countries with more than 5% of poverty are likely to be among the most unhappy, independently of their drug offence rates which can range between 0% for Ghana and 0.12% for Georgia. Tajikistan, Uzbekistan, Nepal, and Colombia appear to be exceptions which report relatively high happiness score despite poverty rate exceeding 6% and relatively low registered offence rate.

However, this kind of police data has its downsides when estimating a country's drug use level using the rate of offences. It reflects the result of the actions of law enforcement institutions, which in their turn depend on political and cultural factors. We can easily imagine that in some countries, "war on drugs" is not a priority. We can also imagine the existence of corrupt law enforcement officers who charge money to avoid imposing court proceedings or official fines on offenders. To see the fuller picture of drug use trends, we need data of different nature, which is the subject of the next section.

Creating Additional Metrics

The World Drug Report 2022 provides us with a spreadsheet containing information about percentage of people having used drugs in the last year broken down by different types of drugs in the spreadsheet 1.2_-_Prevalence_of_drug_use_in_the_general_population_including_NPS_national_data.xlsx provided by UNODC as a part of the Statistical annex to the World Drug Report 2022. I decided to estimate general drug consumption from this data. But what function to use to aggregate the data? Sum is the first that comes to mind. But it has a problem: as people from these surveys could use more than one type of drug, it does not make sense to simply add the percentages across different types of drugs. That is why I decided to take the average and the maximum across types of drugs for the most recent year available to estimate countries' consumption level.

I used Best columns from the sheets on common drugs and Past_Year_Total from the sheet on various so-called NPS (New Psychoactive Substances). Data about NPS is presented on a single sheet, so after deleting all the rows that do not contain my target metric Past_Year_Total, I separated it into one sheet per substance. Per country tables for each common drug type (9) and each NPS (12) became sheets of my summary_drug-poverty-happiness.xlsx. I aggregated data using XLOOKUP on the summary sheet and applied "average" and "maximum" aggregation functions. I named the columns Average_last_year and Max_last_year respectively. I also added a What_is_max column to indicate which drug is the most popular in a given country.

Analysis of Survey Data

Contrary to what we have seen with police data, it is obvious from the distributions in Average Use Last Year vs. Poverty and Happiness and in Max Use Last Year vs. Poverty and Happiness scatter plots that there is no relation between usage and poverty. On the one hand, usage can be high in both poorest countries (like Zambia, Madagascar, Haiti, and Nigeria) and the countries less touched by poverty (for example France, Switzerland, and Ireland), on the other hand, low usage occurs in the poorest countries with more than 10% of extreme poverty (Congo, Turkmenistan, Senegal) and the those with less than 1% of extreme poverty (Turkey, South Korea, Moldova). The same can be seen on the map Average Last Year Usage vs. Poverty, the distribution on which contrasts with maps based on police reports.

Given information from the section on police data, we can conclude that there probably is a relation between police interventions against users and traffickers in poorer and wealthier countries, but there is no relation between poverty and actual drug use. The trend identified in the previous section is rooted in law enforcement practices. The police pays more attention to the drug-related offences in wealthier countries. As for the happiness score and drug use, we can notice that on Average Use Last Year vs. Poverty and Happiness plot there are more green points to the right of the median, which may indicate that higher usage is associated with higher happiness score.

At the same time, there is likely a negative relationship between poverty and happiness. Countries with low poverty rate are more likely to have happier population, as it shows Poverty vs. Happiness plot. We can add as a sidenote that happier countries tend to have higher cannabis use rates, as can be seen on the same plot: the greener the point (light green starting at 0.9%), the higher is the cannabis use rate.

Conclusion

In conclusion, this study analyzed the relationship between drug consumption, drug-related crimes, poverty, and perceived happiness at the country level. The data for this study was gathered from multiple sources and preprocessed in order to properly analyze the correlations between these factors. The results of the analysis showed that there is a negative correlation between happiness and poverty, a positive correlation between happiness and drug-related crimes, and a positive correlation between happiness and drug use. We found no evidence of a link between drug usage and poverty rate. These findings suggest that stricter policies aimed at reducing drug-related crime do not lead to lower usage, while policies aimed at reduction of poverty may lead to increased happiness in the population. These results can inform political decision-makers in their efforts to maximize the happiness of the population.
